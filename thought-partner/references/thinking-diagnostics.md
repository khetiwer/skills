# Thinking State Diagnostics

Reference guide for detecting when the user's reasoning is serving something other than inquiry. Read this when Phase 2 begins to match your challenge to the actual thinking pattern.

Every thinking process serves something. The question is whether it serves truth-seeking or something else — comfort, status, completion, defense.

## Healthy State: Process-Sovereignty

**What's fixed:** Only the commitment to inquiry itself.
**What moves:** Conclusions, methods, beliefs — all move when evidence demands it.
**Signs:** Genuine curiosity. Willingness to say "I was wrong." Engages counterarguments seriously. Seeks disconfirming evidence.
**Your role:** Collaborative partner. Challenge where genuinely productive. Don't manufacture friction.

## GT1: Conclusion-Preservation

**What's fixed:** A specific conclusion the user has already reached.
**What moves:** Everything else bends to defend it.
**Signs:** Evidence for is weighted heavily; evidence against is explained away. "Yes, but..." responses to challenges. Growing elaboration of defense. The question "what would change your mind?" produces discomfort.
**Mechanism:** Identity fusion — the conclusion feels like part of who they are.
**Intervention:** Don't attack the conclusion directly. Decouple it from identity. Ask: "If this turned out to be wrong, what would that mean about you? Probably nothing — let's look at it fresh." Use Inversion: "What would guarantee this conclusion is wrong?"

## GT2: Authority-Preservation

**What's fixed:** Being the expert / being right.
**What moves:** Will change conclusions but won't credit others' reasoning.
**Signs:** "I was already thinking that" pattern. Reframes others' contributions as confirmations. Discomfort when expertise is questioned. Willing to change position only if they can frame it as their own insight.
**Mechanism:** Identity fusion — fused to the role, not the conclusion.
**Intervention:** Don't threaten their expertise. Frame challenges as collaborative exploration: "Let's stress-test this together" rather than "I think you're wrong about..."

## GT3: Threat-Reduction

**What's fixed:** Comfort / safety. Discomfort is the driver.
**What moves:** Resolution is sought for relief, not accuracy. Complexity is misread as danger.
**Signs:** Rushing to resolve ambiguity. Strong preference for simple explanations. "I just need to decide and move on."
**Mechanism:** State activation — stress shifts orientation before deliberation begins.
**Intervention:** Address the state before the content. Slow things down. "There's no pressure to decide right now. Let's hold both options for a moment." Create safety first, then analyze. Apply Hold before Resolve.

## GT4: Completion-Seeking

**What's fixed:** Producing an answer. Output over accuracy.
**What moves:** Quality of analysis.
**Signs:** Quick, confident answers without proportionate analysis. Discomfort with "I don't know." Resistance to reopening "settled" questions. Speed of conclusion inversely proportional to complexity of problem.
**Mechanism:** Habitual pattern or social pressure to have answers.
**Intervention:** Insert deliberate pauses. "Before we settle on this, let me push on it from one angle." Apply Hold before Resolve.

## GT5: Monitor Co-option (Most Dangerous)

**What's fixed:** The defense system itself.
**What moves:** Nothing.
**Signs:** Elaborate justification that always confirms the same conclusion. "I've really thought about this carefully" with zero belief revision. Counter-evidence triggers more analysis, not more doubt. Engagement with counterarguments that somehow always strengthens the original position.
**Mechanism:** Identity fusion operating through the self-monitoring mechanism.
**Intervention:** Do NOT argue content — that feeds the defense. Introduce external checks: "What prediction does this view make that we could verify?" Suggest external feedback, prediction tracking, or literal scorekeeping. Move from internal analysis to testable claims.

## Quick Reference

| State | What's Fixed | First Move |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| GT1 - Conclusion-Preservation | A specific conclusion | Decouple conclusion from identity |
| GT2 - Authority-Preservation | Being the authority | Differentiate authority from accuracy |
| GT3 - Threat-Reduction | Comfort / safety | Address the emotional state first |
| GT4 - Completion-Seeking | Producing output | Hold before Resolve |
| GT5 - Monitor Co-option | The defense itself | External monitoring scaffolding |

## Mechanism-Specific Responses

**Habitual capture (GT4):** No active defense. A prompt to re-evaluate is often sufficient. "Let's step back and check if this approach still fits."

**Identity fusion (GT1, GT2, GT5):** The most persistent. Cannot be fixed by "just think harder" — that strengthens the defense. Requires decoupling from identity, plus external monitoring. Frame challenges as exploring the idea, not attacking the person.

**State activation (GT3):** Physiological state shifted orientation before deliberation. Address the state first — create safety, reduce pressure, add time. Only then re-evaluate the thinking.
